relating to: warning labels for explicit 2content and providing a penalty.Analysis by the Legislative Reference BureauThis bill requires distributors of explicit content on digital platforms to provide prominent, clear, and conspicuous warning labels. “Explicit content” means visual, written, or auditory material that is harmful to children, which is defined under current law to mean that quality of any description, narrative account, or representation of nudity, sexually explicit conduct, sexual excitement, sadomasochistic abuse, physical torture, or brutality if it 1) predominantly appeals to the prurient, shameful, or morbid interest of children, 2) is patently offensive to prevailing standards in the adult community as a whole with respect to what is suitable for children, and 3) lacks serious literary, artistic, political, scientific, or educational value for children, when taken as a whole. The bill defines a “distributor” as a person that provides, sells, or publishes explicit content for profit or as part of a commercial service wherein one-third or more of the material is explicit content, not including a broadband internet access service or telecommunications service. The warning label for explicit content must use language that is substantially similar to the following: “WARNING: This material contains explicit content that may be harmful or offensive. Viewer discretion is advised. Not intended for minors.” A distributor of explicit content on a digital platform must provide the warning label in high-contrast, bolded text and the warning label must 1) appear before the user accesses the explicit content and remain visible to the user for at least 10 seconds or until the user acknowledges the warning label through interaction with the digital platform; and 2) occupy at least two-thirds of the user’s screen. The distributor must also ensure that each piece of explicit content it provides contains in the explicit content’s metadata “WisconsinExplicitContentWarning.”The Department of Justice must investigate violations of the bill’s provisions, and DOJ may bring an action to recover a forfeiture or for injunctive relief. Before DOJ initiates an action, DOJ must provide the distributor with notice identifying the alleged violation. For an initial violation, a distributor is subject to a forfeiture of up to $2,500, except that, if the distributor cures the violation within 14 days of receiving the DOJ notice, the distributor is subject to a forfeiture of up to $500. For a second or subsequent violation, a distributor is subject to a forfeiture of twice the amount of the forfeiture imposed on the distributor for the most recent prior violation.The people of the state of Wisconsin, represented in senate and assembly, do enact as follows:AB961-ASA3,11Section 1. 100.77 of the statutes is created to read:AB961-ASA3,2,32100.77 Warning labels for explicit content.

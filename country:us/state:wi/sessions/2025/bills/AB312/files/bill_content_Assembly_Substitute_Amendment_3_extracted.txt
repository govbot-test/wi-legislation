to create 6.855 (6), 6.855 (7), 6.86 (1) (bm), 216.2972, 16.2975, 20.505 (1) (eg) and 20.505 (1) (ej) of the statutes; relating 3to: hours for in-person absentee voting at the office of the municipal clerk or 4an alternate site and related municipality reimbursements, allowing 5designation of the county clerk’s office as an alternate absentee ballot site, 6allowing adjacent municipalities to jointly designate an alternate absentee 7ballot site, and election security grants.Analysis by the Legislative Reference BureauIn-person absentee voting hours; municipality reimbursementsUnder current law, a voter may vote by absentee ballot in person at the office of the municipal clerk or at an alternate site as near as practicable to the clerk’s office, as designated by the municipality. The period for voting absentee in person begins 14 days preceding the election and ends on the Sunday preceding the election, and the municipality must state the hours in the type E election notice, which, with one exception, must be published on the fourth Tuesday preceding each primary or election.This bill requires the office of the municipal clerk or alternate absentee ballot site to be open for in-person absentee voting for at least a certain number of hours on the basis of the number of registered voters in the municipality, as follows:1. A municipality having fewer than 600 registered voters must be open for at least 10 hours for in-person absentee voting.2. A municipality having between 600 and 799 registered voters must be open for at least 15 hours.3. A municipality having 800 or more registered voters must be open for at least 20 hours.These requirements apply for the election held on the first Tuesday in April each year (spring election) and the November general election. Also, if two or more municipalities share a common location for their municipal clerk’s office or alternate site, the number of hours required under the bill for the largest of those municipalities controls for purposes of requirements specified above.The bill additionally requires the Department of Administration to reimburse municipalities at a rate of $50 per hour for the number of hours the municipality’s municipal clerk’s office or alternate site is required under the bill to remain open for a spring election that exceed the number of hours the municipal clerk’s office or alternate site remained open in the 2024 spring election, and likewise for the general election.However, under the bill, a municipality is not required to maintain the certain number office hours described above for purposes of in-person absentee voting during any spring or general election for which sufficient moneys are not made available to fund the DOA reimbursements. The bill requires the secretary of administration to provide advance notice to municipalities before each spring and general election stating whether sufficient moneys are available to fund the reimbursements for that election and if not, that the office-hours requirements under the bill do not apply to that election.Alternate absentee ballot sitesThe bill provides that if a municipality is unable to comply with the hour requirements for in-person absentee voting, as described above, two adjacent municipalities may designate an alternate absentee ballot site to serve the voters of both municipalities. The bill also allows the governing bodies of both municipalities to designate the office of one of the municipal clerks to serve as an alternate site to serve the voters of both municipalities. If two adjacent municipalities jointly designate an alternate absentee ballot site, the municipalities must enter into a memorandum of understanding that acknowledges that the designation is a voluntary agreement with the approval of the governing body of each municipality. The memorandum must also identify the requirements of each municipal clerk for operating the alternate absentee ballot site, including security protocols and the responsibility for communicating with voters.Under the bill, if a municipality is unable to comply with the hour requirements for in-person absentee voting and unable to jointly designate an alternate absentee ballot site with an adjacent municipality, the municipality, with the consent of the county board and county clerk where the municipality is located, may designate the office of the county clerk as an alternate absentee ballot site, if the office of the county clerk is located no more than 25 miles from the municipal clerk’s office. Under the bill, the alternate site of the county clerk’s office may be staffed by the county clerk, municipal clerk, or executive director of the board of election commissioners or by the employees of the county clerk, municipal clerk, or board of election commissioners.The bill requires the governing body of the municipality and the county board and county clerk to enter into a memorandum of understanding that acknowledges that the designation of the county clerk’s office as an alternate absentee ballot site is made as a voluntary agreement with the approval of the governing body of the municipality, county board, and county clerk. The memorandum must also identify the requirements for the office of the county clerk to operate as an alternate absentee ballot site, including security protocols and the responsibility for communicating with voters.The bill requires the office of the municipal clerk of a municipality that enters into a memorandum of understanding with another municipality or with the county to itself be open for in-person absentee voting at the spring election or general election for at least the same number of hours that the office of the municipal clerk was open for that purpose for the 2024 spring election or general election, as applicable. Similarly, if a municipality that enters into a memorandum of understanding with another municipality or with the county designated an alternate absentee ballot site for the 2024 spring election or general election, the municipality must designate at least one alternate site located within the municipality, as near as practicable to the office of the municipal clerk, which must be open for in-person absentee voting at the spring election or general election for at least the same number of hours that the alternate site designated for 2024 was open for that purpose for the 2024 spring election or general election, as applicable.Election security grantsThe bill establishes a grant program administered by the Department of Administration to award grants to counties and municipalities to pay for certain security measures to be implemented with regard to polling locations and central count locations in buildings owned by or leased to a county or municipality. Under the bill, for example, a county or municipality may receive a grant to pay for panic buttons, electronic access key cards, surveillance cameras, metal detectors, and secure ballot containers. Under the bill, a grant recipient receives an amount equal to 50 percent of the cost to implement a specified security measure.The bill also allows a county to receive a grant to pay 50 percent of the cost to enter into an agreement with a federally recognized program or a certified cybersecurity vendor to deploy an intrusion detection and network monitoring system to detect and mitigate threats to the election systems in each municipality located within the county. The grant may also be used to pay 50 percent of the costs to operate the system for the first 12 months in which the system is operational.The people of the state of Wisconsin, represented in senate and assembly, do enact as follows:AB312-ASA3,11Section 1. 6.855 (6) of the statutes is created to read:AB312-ASA3,2,1326.855 (6) (a) If a municipality is unable to comply with the requirements 3under s. 6.86 (1) (bm), the governing bodies of two adjacent municipalities may 4jointly designate an alternate site under sub.
